Internet Service Providers

Overview

An Internet Service Provider (ISP) is an organization that provides services for accessing, using, or participating in the Internet. ISPs form the commercial backbone of Internet connectivity, operating the infrastructure that enables end users and organizations to connect to the global network. Understanding ISP operations is essential for network planning, troubleshooting connectivity issues, and comprehending how data traverses the Internet.

The Internet is not a single network but rather an interconnection of thousands of independent networks operated by ISPs worldwide. These networks must cooperate and exchange traffic through defined relationships to create the unified global network we call the Internet. The technical and business relationships between ISPs fundamentally shape Internet performance, resilience, and accessibility.

Internet Service Provider (ISP)
An organization that provides customers with access to the Internet and related services. ISPs maintain network infrastructure, routing systems, and interconnections with other networks to enable global connectivity.

ISP Tier Classification

ISPs are classified into tiers based on their role in the Internet hierarchy and their interconnection relationships with other providers. This tier system reflects both network size and the provider's position within the global routing infrastructure.

Tier 1 ISPs

Tier 1 providers represent the apex of the Internet hierarchy. These organizations operate massive global networks with direct connections to every part of the Internet. The defining characteristic of a Tier 1 provider is that they can reach every other network on the Internet without purchasing transit from any other provider. Tier 1 networks exchange traffic exclusively through settlement-free peering agreements with other Tier 1 providers.

Tier 1 ISP Characteristics
  • Global Reach: Direct or peered connectivity to all Internet destinations
  • Transit-Free: Does not purchase transit from any other provider
  • Peering: Settlement-free peering with all other Tier 1 providers
  • Infrastructure: Operates intercontinental backbone networks
  • AS Numbers: Manages significant IP address and AS resources
  • Examples: Lumen (CenturyLink), NTT, Cogent, GTT, Telia

Tier 2 ISPs

Tier 2 providers operate regional or national networks that require a combination of peering and transit to reach all Internet destinations. These ISPs have extensive networks but lack the global reach of Tier 1 providers. They peer with some networks directly while purchasing transit from Tier 1 providers for destinations they cannot reach through peering.

Tier 2 ISP Characteristics
  • Regional/National Scope: Significant but not global infrastructure
  • Transit Required: Purchases transit for some destinations
  • Peering Active: Maintains numerous peering relationships
  • Customer Base: Serves smaller ISPs and enterprise customers
  • Examples: Regional carriers, national ISPs without global backbone

Tier 3 ISPs

Tier 3 providers are typically local or retail ISPs that purchase all their transit from larger providers. These organizations focus on last-mile connectivity, delivering Internet access to residential and small business customers within a specific geographic area. Tier 3 ISPs do not typically peer with other networks; they rely entirely on their upstream transit providers for Internet connectivity.

Tier 3 ISP Characteristics
  • Local Scope: Serves specific geographic region
  • Transit Dependent: Purchases all upstream connectivity
  • No Peering: Limited or no direct peering relationships
  • Focus: Last-mile delivery to end consumers
  • Examples: Local cable providers, regional DSL operators

Interconnection Types

ISPs interconnect through two primary relationship types: peering and transit. Understanding these relationships is critical for comprehending Internet routing, performance optimization, and traffic engineering.

Peering

Peering is an agreement between two networks to exchange traffic between their respective customers directly. In a peering arrangement, neither party pays the other for traffic exchange. Peering is typically established when both parties benefit roughly equally from the direct traffic exchange, reducing the need for transit.

Settlement-Free Peering
An interconnection agreement where two networks exchange traffic without monetary payment. Each party carries traffic destined for its own customers at its own expense. This arrangement assumes roughly equal benefit to both parties.

Peering can be established through two methods:

Type Description Characteristics
Private Peering Direct physical connection between two networks Dedicated capacity, higher cost, greater control
Public Peering Connection through an Internet Exchange Point (IXP) Shared facility, lower cost, access to multiple peers

Transit

Transit is a paid service where one network pays another for access to the entire Internet. The transit provider agrees to forward traffic from the customer network to any destination on the Internet and to accept traffic from anywhere on the Internet destined for the customer. Transit providers announce the customer's routes to their peers and upstream providers.

Internet Exchange Points

An Internet Exchange Point (IXP) is a physical location where multiple networks interconnect to exchange traffic. IXPs provide shared switching infrastructure that enables efficient peering between many networks at reduced cost compared to establishing individual private peering connections.

IXP Functions
  • Traffic Localization: Keeps local traffic local, reducing latency
  • Cost Reduction: Shared infrastructure lowers interconnection costs
  • Performance: Direct paths between networks improve latency
  • Resilience: Multiple peering options provide redundancy
  • Neutrality: Typically carrier-neutral facilities

Major IXPs handle enormous traffic volumes. The largest exchanges process multiple terabits per second of aggregate traffic, facilitating efficient routing between hundreds of member networks.

Last Mile Technologies

The last mile refers to the final segment of network infrastructure connecting the ISP's distribution network to the end customer's premises. Last mile technology directly impacts the bandwidth, latency, and reliability experienced by end users.

Technology Medium Typical Speed Characteristics
DSL Copper telephone line 1-100 Mbps Distance-sensitive, widely available
Cable (DOCSIS) Coaxial cable 10-1000 Mbps Shared medium, asymmetric typical
Fiber (FTTH) Fiber optic 100-10000 Mbps Highest bandwidth, low latency
Fixed Wireless Radio frequency 10-1000 Mbps Line-of-sight, weather-sensitive
Satellite Radio (orbital) 10-200 Mbps High latency (GEO), global coverage
Cellular (5G) Radio frequency 50-1000+ Mbps Mobile, shared spectrum

DSL Technologies

Digital Subscriber Line (DSL) transmits data over existing copper telephone infrastructure. DSL utilizes frequency ranges above voice frequencies, enabling simultaneous voice and data transmission. Performance degrades with distance from the central office, limiting practical deployment range.

DSL Variants
  • ADSL: Asymmetric, higher download than upload (8/1 Mbps typical)
  • ADSL2+: Enhanced ADSL, up to 24 Mbps downstream
  • VDSL: Very high bit rate DSL, up to 52 Mbps
  • VDSL2: Up to 100 Mbps, requires fiber closer to premises
  • G.fast: Up to 1 Gbps, requires fiber within 250 meters

Fiber Technologies

Fiber optic last mile connections represent the highest-performance access technology currently deployed at scale. Fiber to the Home (FTTH) delivers fiber directly to the customer premises, providing symmetric multi-gigabit capability with extremely low latency.

ISP Routing Operations

ISPs utilize the Border Gateway Protocol (BGP) to exchange routing information with peers and customers. BGP enables ISPs to advertise the networks they can reach and to learn routes from other networks. The aggregation of all BGP-exchanged routes forms the global Internet routing table.

BGP Overview
  • Protocol Type: Path-vector routing protocol
  • Transport: TCP port 179
  • Scope: Inter-domain (between Autonomous Systems)
  • Selection: Based on policy, AS path, and other attributes
  • Convergence: Slow compared to IGPs (minutes)
  • Table Size: 900,000+ prefixes in global table (2024)

Autonomous Systems

An Autonomous System (AS) is a collection of IP networks under the control of a single administrative entity that presents a common routing policy to the Internet. Each AS is identified by a unique Autonomous System Number (ASN), assigned by Regional Internet Registries.

Section Summary

This section covered the structure and operations of Internet Service Providers:

  • Three-tier ISP hierarchy: Tier 1 (global), Tier 2 (regional), Tier 3 (local)
  • Interconnection types: Peering (mutual benefit) and Transit (paid service)
  • Internet Exchange Points for efficient multi-party peering
  • Last mile technologies: DSL, Cable, Fiber, Wireless, Satellite
  • BGP as the inter-domain routing protocol
  • Autonomous Systems as administrative routing domains
Key Takeaways
  • The Internet is an interconnection of thousands of independent networks
  • Tier 1 ISPs form the backbone with global transit-free reach
  • Peering reduces costs; transit provides full Internet access
  • IXPs enable efficient traffic exchange between networks
  • Last mile technology directly impacts end-user experience
  • BGP governs all inter-domain routing on the Internet
